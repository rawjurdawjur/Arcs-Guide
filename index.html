How the Draft Works

With Leaders & Lore in the mix, setup replaces the symmetric start: a pool of Leader and Lore cards is revealed and drafted in reverse turn order, so the player going last picks first. Everyone ends up with exactly one Leader and one Lore card (the Archivist being the famous exception — she leaves setup holding three Lore). Your Leader rewrites your starting buildings and resources and gives you a permanent upside stapled to a permanent downside; your Lore is a private technology that no one else has — and unlike Guild cards, Lore has no guild icon and no raid cost, so it never adds to ambitions and can never be stolen. It is yours for the whole game.

Because the draft is combinatorial, evaluate the pool as pairs, not singles. A mid-tier Lore next to the right Leader (Raider Exosuits next to the Corsair, Force Beams next to the Rebel, Tycoon's Charm next to the Mystic) jumps a full tier. When you pick first, take the strongest single card; when you pick in the middle, take the card that completes your pair or breaks up an opponent's.

Five Principles Before You Pick

1. Downsides are the real text. Every Leader's upside is fun; the downside is what actually shapes your game. "Gain no Power for second place" clauses (Elder, Warrior, Archivist, Noble) quietly forbid you from doing what wins most Arcs games — banking cheap seconds — so those Leaders must commit to firsts.

2. Copy/Pivot riders are action economy. Several Leaders trigger only when you Copy or Pivot into an action (Mystic, Fuel-Drinker, Agitator taxing; Warrior battling; Feastbringer securing; Noble influencing). These reward following rather than leading tricks — they make your off-suit turns nearly as good as your on-suit ones, which changes how you value initiative.

3. Ambition-gated Lore needs a declarer. The eight "While X is declared" Lore cards from the pack work whenever anyone has declared that ambition — but you can't rely on rivals to declare on schedule. They're strongest with Leaders who declare often (Upstart, Demagogue, Noble) or whose economy naturally feeds that ambition.

4. Fresh matters. A surprising share of Lore keys off fresh ships and buildings (Signal Breaker, Seeker Torpedoes, Sprinter Drives, Hidden Harbors, Predictive Sensors, Railgun Arrays, Force Beams). Repair actions and building fresh in contested space are worth more with these in play.

5. Your Lore is permanent — most of what it produces isn't. Lore has no raid cost, so no rival can ever take it from you. But the resources and Guild cards your Lore engine generates are raidable, and a strong engine makes its output the juiciest target at the table. Draft the engine freely; budget for defending its dividends.

Leaders — Base Game

The eight Leaders included in the Arcs base box. Card text is quoted exactly; setup shows the starting buildings on your A and B planets and your two starting resources.

Elder

Base GameDefensive · CourtModerate difficulty

Beloved. After defending in battle, you may influence if the attacker took any Trophies. Rivals cannot Ransack the Court when they battle you.

Just. When you're scoring Tyrant, gain no Power for second place, and gain Power for second place if you get first place. (Don't get bonus city Power.)

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Relic Material

How to play it

The Elder wants to be attacked — every battle that costs you a Trophy pays you back with a free influence, and no one can Ransack your Court agents by killing your pieces. That combination makes the classic anti-turtle tools much weaker against you, so lean into it: park pieces where they're tempting, spend your real actions on the Court, and let aggressive rivals convert their Aggression cards into your board presence. You are the best pure Court player in the base box.

Just reads like a penalty but is really a fork: either ignore Tyrant entirely, or win it outright and collect both place payouts. If you drift into Tyrant, commit — a second-place Tyrant position is dead Power for you. The Relic start also nudges you toward Keeper, which suits a low-conflict, Court-heavy game perfectly.

Best lore pairings

Mirror Plating — punishes the attacks you're inviting Railgun Arrays — a hit-tax on anyone who tests you Keeper's Trust — locks down the Relic hoard Predictive Sensors — turns bait into ambush

Mystic

Base GameEconomic · EmpathHarder than it looks

Attuned. When you Copy or Pivot in order to tax, you gain 1 Psionic along with the taxed resource.

Cryptic. In setup, place agents on your Material and Fuel Outrage slots on your player board.

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Psionic Relic

How to play it

Starting the game Outraged on Material and Fuel is the harshest setup clause in the box — your two most common resources lose their Prelude actions before the first card is played. The compensation is that every Copy/Pivot tax mints a Psionic, and Psionic is the wildcard resource: spend it in your Prelude to take any action your played card allows. So the Mystic converts a boring resource economy into a flexible one: tax constantly on off-suit turns, stockpile Psionics, and use them as universal action pips while feeding a monstrous Empath position.

Declare Empath early and repeatedly — you'll usually win it by a mile, and your rivals must burn actions taxing Psionic planets just to contest second. Your real weakness is tempo in chapters where you hold initiative: leading a trick gives you no Attuned trigger, so the Mystic often prefers to follow. Losing Material's build Prelude also means your infrastructure grows only through Construction cards; protect your starport.

Best lore pairings

Tycoon's Charm — its discard Prelude erases Cryptic entirely Empath's Vision — you'll have Empath declared anyway Empath's Bond — tax the whole map while Empath is up Ancient Holdings — a vault slot for the Psionic pile
Official ruling Attuned still grants the Psionic even if the tax itself produced no resource — the "along with" wording only clarifies that it doesn't replace the taxed resource.

Fuel-Drinker

Base GameEconomic · MobilityBeginner-friendly

Insatiable. When you Copy or Pivot in order to tax, you gain 1 Fuel along with the taxed resource.

Lavish. After scoring, you must discard all of your Fuel if the Tycoon ambition was scored.

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Fuel Fuel

How to play it

Every off-suit tax pumps Fuel into your play area, and Fuel is mobility: its Prelude action is a free Move. Played correctly the Fuel-Drinker is the most mobile faction on the table, repositioning before every real action and appearing wherever the scoring is. The trap is hoarding. Lavish fires whenever Tycoon is scored by anyone, so a fat Fuel pile is a hostage — a rival can declare Tycoon, ride it to chapter end, and torch your savings.

Two honest lines: either spend it — treat Fuel as motion, keep two or three in reserve, and never sit on a pile into a scoring — or win it, declaring Tycoon yourself early, riding your natural Fuel income to first place, and accepting the bonfire after you've banked the Power. Either way, watch which chapters have Tycoon declared and time your taxes accordingly.

Best lore pairings

Tycoon's Charm — launder Fuel into safe resources before scoring Tycoon's Ambition — the Fuel was going to burn anyway; buy a declare with it Sprinter Drives — Fuel moves plus double movement is absurd reach Gate Ports — mobility loves owning the crossroads

Upstart

Base GameTempo · FlexibleBeginner-friendly

Ambitious. When you declare an ambition, you may gain any resource. (You must declare the ambition before taking any actions, including Prelude actions from resources.)

Callow. You can only tax Loyal cities if you control them.

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Psionic Material

How to play it

The community's consensus beginner pick, and still strong in veteran hands. Each declaration hands you any resource on demand — and because the declare happens before your Prelude, the resource is live immediately. Declare Warlord, take a Weapon, spend it to battle that same turn; declare Tycoon, take the Material or Fuel that pads your own count. The Upstart wants initiative: lead 7s, seize when it's cheap, and declare something almost every round. Even a second-place ambition finish is fine when the declare itself paid you.

Callow (see the errata below) means your home economy only works where you dominate. Keep your starting clusters genuinely controlled — extra ships on your own planets aren't wasted — and don't scatter thin. The Psionic start gives you one wildcard turn early; save it for the moment a tempo swing wins you an ambition race.

Best lore pairings

Tycoon's Ambition — chained declares, each one paying a resource Ancient Holdings — six slots for the free income Tyrant's Authority — control is your whole game; annex what you hold Warlord's Cruelty — you declare Warlord often enough to use it
Errata Callow now reads: "You cannot tax cities that you do not control." Same intent, cleaner interaction with cards that let you tax non-Loyal cities.

Rebel

Base GameAggressive · RaidingBeginner-friendly

Committed. In battle, you collect 2 extra dice.

Disorganized. You cannot move more than 2 ships at a time.

Setup Single starport on planet A (no second building) · Material Weapon

How to play it

Two ships, four dice. The Rebel's entire identity is the kamikaze raid: slide a pair of fresh ships next to something valuable and attack with double the dice anyone else would get from the same force. You will trade pieces constantly and come out ahead on Trophies, stolen resources, and stolen cards almost every time. Warlord is your natural ambition; raid dice against players fat with Guild cards are your economy.

Disorganized caps every move at two ships — including Catapult moves — so you can never mass a fleet or redeploy across the map in one action. Compensate structurally: build starports forward so reinforcements are born where the fighting is, keep ships pre-spread rather than pooled, and think of your military as many two-ship cells rather than one army. Your single starting building also means an early raid on your home can hurt; don't leave it naked.

Best lore pairings

Force Beams — Guide ignores move modifiers, sidestepping Disorganized entirely Signal Breaker — all-fresh two-ship strikes shrug off an intercept Seeker Torpedoes — rerolls make small attacks reliable Raider Exosuits — raid turtles who hide without buildings

Warrior

Base GameAggressive · TempoBeginner-friendly

Tactical. When you Copy or Pivot to battle, you may move before or after you battle. (You may do this even if you spent a Weapon to let you battle.)

Violent. When you're scoring Empath, gain no Power for second place, and gain Power for second place if you get first place. (Don't get bonus city Power.)

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Weapon Material

How to play it

Every off-suit battle comes with a free move stapled to it — move in and strike, or strike and withdraw. That's hit-and-run warfare with none of the usual two-action cost, and it means an Aggression card in someone else's led suit is nearly as good for you as leading it yourself. Even a spent Weapon Prelude battle carries the rider, so a single Weapon resource is a full "move + battle" turn in your pocket.

Use the free move for economy as much as violence: consolidate survivors after a fight, reposition onto a taxing target, or escape a counterattack window. Violent just tells you to stay out of the Empath race unless you can win it outright — with a Weapon-flavored start, you weren't going there anyway. One of the smoothest, most intuitive Leaders in the game.

Best lore pairings

Repair Drones — sustained aggression without attrition Sprinter Drives — the free move moves fresh ships again Seeker Torpedoes — reliable assaults on every strike Warlord's Terror — convert surplus Trophies into Court presence
Official ruling The extra move granted by Tactical is the stated action only — you can't use it to trigger a different action printed on a Guild or Lore card, though modifiers like Sprinter Drives still apply.

Feastbringer

Base GameCourt · PoliticalAdvanced

Charismatic. When you Copy or Pivot in order to secure a card, you may influence before or after you secure.

Generous. To declare an ambition, you must give 1 Guild card to your Rival with the least Power.

Setup Cities on both planets A and B (no starport) · Relic Material

How to play it

Influence-then-secure in a single action is the strongest Court tempo in the game: you can snipe a card you have zero agents on, or top up a contested card and take it in the same breath. The Feastbringer should live in the Court, harvesting Guild cards faster than anyone — which is exactly why the game makes you pay them out. Every declaration ships one Guild card to the trailing player.

Manage Generous three ways: declare rarely and only at full value; keep a shelf of low-impact "gift" Guilds (a spare Union, an Outraged-suit card you can't use) so the tax never costs a card you love; and think about who is last — sometimes the gift steers the table, and sometimes you'd rather let a rival declare the ambition you wanted than feed the wrong player. No Guild card in hand means no declaring at all, so never run the shelf to zero. Two starting cities and no starport means your fleet grows slowly; buy or build a starport early.

Best lore pairings

Tyrant's Ego — captives become extra secures for the machine Keeper's Solidarity — protects the Guild engine you're building Warlord's Terror — another influence faucet for Court dominance Ancient Holdings — Relic start, Keeper lean, protected slot

Demagogue

Base GameCourt · TempoModerate difficulty

Bold. When you declare an ambition, you may influence any number of cards in the Court once.

Paranoid. You cannot secure Guild cards with only 1 Loyal agent on them. (You can secure Vox cards as normal.) Ignore this if you Ransack the Court.

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Psionic Weapon

How to play it

One declaration, one agent on every card in the Court. That's the biggest single burst of action compression any Leader offers — a whole Mobilization card's worth of influence for free, several times a game. The Demagogue should declare early each chapter (the ambition is worth more, and the blanket lands before rivals contest the Court) and then spend real actions doubling up on the two or three cards that matter, because Paranoid demands a second agent before you can secure anything.

Your constraint is the agent supply itself: blanketing the Court plus double-stacking targets burns through agents fast, and every captive a rival takes off a Court card is one you can't redeploy. Watch for Court Enforcers and Tyrant players who feast on your spread agents — sometimes the right play is a narrow blanket, hitting only the cards you actually intend to take. Battling a fat Court player and Ransacking ignores Paranoid entirely; keep that release valve in mind.

Best lore pairings

Tycoon's Ambition — a second declare means a second blanket Warlord's Terror — even more influence from your Trophies Tyrant's Ego — captives convert into the secures Paranoid taxes Keeper's Solidarity — guards the card pile you'll accumulate

Leaders — Leaders & Lore Pack

The eight Leaders added by the expansion pack — generally swingier and more rules-bending than the base eight.

Archivist

L&L PackCombo · VariableAdvanced

Learned. After setup, gain 2 extra lore cards — draw 5 lore, keep 2, and scrap the other 3 (returning them to the box).

Academic. When you're scoring Tycoon, gain no Power for second place, and gain Power for second place if you get first place. (Don't get bonus city Power.)

Setup Cities on both planets A and B (no starport) · Relic Relic

How to play it

Three Lore cards when everyone else has one. The Archivist is the most variable Leader in the game — some draws hand you a broken engine (two defensive Lore stacked, or a "While declared" pair sharing one ambition), others hand you three curiosities. Draft accordingly: since Learned resolves after the draft, pick a reliable, always-useful Lore in the draft itself (Sprinter Drives, Living Structures, Ancient Holdings) and let the five-card draw provide the spice. When the draw offers a pair that shares a trigger — both Empath Lore, both defensive-battle Lore — take the pair over two individually stronger cards.

Two Relics and two cities lean you toward Keeper and a quiet, developmental game while your extra rules compound. And because Lore has no raid cost, all three cards are permanently yours — no rival can ever raid your library away, so build your whole plan on it with confidence. Academic is easy to obey — just don't chase Tycoon seconds.

Best lore pairings

Living Structures — flexible value in any draw Keeper's Trust — your Relic start wants Keeper anyway Railgun Arrays + Mirror Plating — the stacked-defense dream draw Hidden Harbors — protects a play area worth triple raiding
Official ruling Leader setup steps happen after all drafting is complete — you know your drafted Lore before you draw the extra five.

Overseer

L&L PackEconomic · EngineBeginner-friendly

Ruthless. Once per turn, when you tax any city or build a ship at a starport, you may hit the building to take that action again with it. If you destroy a Loyal city, you Provoke Outrage. (If you destroy a Rival city, Provoke Outrage and Ransack the Court.)

Hated. In setup, scrap 2 Loyal ships and 3 Loyal agents. (Return them to the box.)

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Fuel Material

How to play it

The Overseer runs the Reach like a strip mine: one tax becomes two taxes, one build becomes two ships, and the cost is a damage marker on your own infrastructure. The rhythm is squeeze-then-repair — double-dip during Administration and Construction rounds, patch the buildings when a Repair pip comes around, and repeat. Because the damage lands on the building you used, a fresh city is effectively worth an extra resource on demand, which makes your economy the fastest in the pack per action spent.

The advanced tech is squeezing Rival cities you control: their building takes the damage, you take the resources — and if you push one all the way to destruction, you Ransack the Court on top of the Outrage. Just make sure the Outrage type is one you can live with. Hated is fully paid at setup, which is why this Leader teaches well: three fewer agents means a thinner Court game and fewer Outrage buffers, so pick your Court battles and don't spread thin.

Best lore pairings

Living Structures — Nurture is a tax, so Ruthless doubles it Warlord's Cruelty — re-tax stacked on re-tax Cloud Cities — more cities to squeeze Gate Stations — a wildcard-type city to double-tax
Official ruling Ruthless does not require (or spend) another action — it repeats the effect of the action you just took; the only cost is the hit to the building.

Corsair

L&L PackAggressive · RaidingExpert — punishing

Tricky. When attacking in battle, you may reroll raid dice up to the number of different resources you have.

Wary. When attacking in battle, you cannot collect more assault dice than skirmish dice.

Setup Single starport on planet A with an enlarged home fleet (no second building) · Fuel Weapon

How to play it

The raid specialist. Raid dice are the swingiest dice in Arcs — keys steal cards and resources, but the blowback faces hurt — and Tricky lets you reroll them, scaled to how diversified your hoard is. A Corsair holding four different resource types rerolls four raid dice per battle, which turns raiding from a gamble into a business. Deliberately collect one of everything before you start plundering; that diversity is your dice insurance. Then hunt the players fat with Guild cards and resources rather than the ones with big fleets.

Wary means you can never stack pure assault: every assault die must be matched by a skirmish die, so your battles need bodies, not just intent. This is the Leader the community warns against handing to new or hyper-aggressive players — misplayed, the raid blowback and the assault cap grind you into dust. Played patiently, with picked fights against undefended loot, the Corsair converts other people's economies into yours.

Best lore pairings

Raider Exosuits — raid dice against buildingless targets; the signature combo Ancient Holdings — a sixth slot helps hold one of everything Empath's Vision — a second layer of rerolls on everything Repair Drones — keeps the raiding fleet alive between hauls
Official ruling Reroll abilities trigger one at a time, but all rerolls from a single ability happen simultaneously — you can't reroll Tricky's dice one by one.

Noble

L&L PackCourt · High riskExpert

Connected. When you declare an ambition that's not declared, draw and secure the top card of the Court deck. (You can't use its Prelude action now.)

Influential. When you Copy or Pivot to influence, you may influence twice.

Proud. When scoring, you only gain Power if you get first place (not tied).

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B · Psionic Psionic

How to play it

Two strong upsides bolted to the harshest scoring clause in the game. Free Court cards fall into your lap every time you're first to declare an ambition, and your off-suit influence is doubled — the Noble accumulates Guild cards and Court presence faster than anyone without spending a single extra action. But Proud means second places, and even ties for first, are worth zero. Every ambition you touch must be an outright win or it's charity for the table.

So play the Noble like a sniper: pick one or two ambitions per chapter you can genuinely dominate, declare them first (banking the free card), and use the doubled influence plus your Guild engine to make first place mathematically safe before scoring. Fold instantly from races you can't win — the Power you don't waste chasing seconds is the margin you'll win by. The blind secure is also a Tyrant liability: cards with Rival agents on them arrive with captives attached, which is either a gift or a target on your back. A fan-favorite for players who like living dangerously.

Best lore pairings

Tycoon's Ambition — extra first-declares, extra free Court cards Keeper's Solidarity — insures the growing card pile Empath's Vision — double Psionic start makes Empath your lockable ambition Ancient Holdings — hoard quietly, win the count outright
Official ruling If Connected and another declare trigger (like Farseers) fire together, you choose the order.

Anarchist

L&L PackEconomic · ParasiteAdvanced

Decentralized. In setup, scrap your 2 leftmost cities from your player board.

Inspiring. In systems with Loyal ships, you may tax Rival cities ignoring control, and you may tax empty building slots like Loyal cities.

Principled. You cannot tax Loyal cities.

Remember, if you have no buildings on the map, Rivals can raid your ships!

Setup No starting buildings — ships only · Relic Weapon

How to play it

The Anarchist owns nothing and taxes everything. A single Loyal ship in a system unlocks every Rival city there — no control required — plus any empty building slot, taxed as if it were your own. Your economy is therefore the whole map: spread ships wide and thin, one per juicy system, and let other players build your tax base for you. Taxing Rival cities also takes captives, so a sprawling Anarchist quietly assembles a monstrous Tyrant position while looking like a pacifist.

The discipline is remembering what you can't do: your own Loyal cities are economically dead (Principled), so build them only for control, defense, and city-bonus Power — or don't build them at all and pour Construction into starports. Starting with zero buildings has a sharp edge: until you build something, every fight against you offers raid dice against your ships. Get one building on the map early. The Relic-and-Weapon start gives you a Keeper seed and a battle Prelude for the moment someone objects to being farmed.

Best lore pairings

Warlord's Cruelty — double-tax the Rival cities you're farming Force Beams — reposition the swarm (and its victims) Tool-Priests — build ships at the Rival cities you control Sprinter Drives — spread the swarm twice as fast
Official ruling Principled applies only to cities that are actually Loyal — taxing Rival cities "like they are Loyal" (via Inspiring or Empath's Bond) is fine, but genuinely Loyal cities stay off-limits even if another card would permit it.

Shaper

L&L PackEconomic · Map controlAdvanced

Mythic. After you tax a city, you may place 1 resource you have over the planet's resource icon. From now on, its planet type is the placed resource, and its planet type cannot be changed again with Mythic.

Ancient. You cannot Catapult move from starports, but you can Catapult move from gates.

Setup Single city on planet A (no starport), with extra ships staged at the cluster gate · Relic Material

How to play it

The Shaper literally rewrites the map. Each tax lets you overwrite that planet's type with a resource from your hand — permanently, for everyone. Two great uses: farm-building, converting your home cluster into Psionic or Relic planets so your taxes feed Empath or Keeper directly (types nobody can produce on demand); and denial, overwriting the Material or Fuel belt a Tycoon rival depends on, or converting a planet so its Outrage type changes under someone's feet. Spend cheap resources to mint expensive planets — placing a Material to create a Psionic world is a profitable trade forever.

Ancient is a genuinely strange movement profile: your starports build ships but can't Catapult, while every gate on the map is a launch pad. So the Shaper fights for the center — ships parked on gates redeploy en masse in any direction, and your whole military posture should orbit the gate network rather than your home systems. It also means a boxed-in Shaper with no gate access is slow; keep a lane to the middle open at all costs.

Best lore pairings

Gate Stations — cities on the gates you already live on Gate Ports — starports on gates, fully Catapult-legal for you Empath's Bond — tax the farms you terraform, wherever they are Living Structures — more taxes, more Mythic triggers

Agitator

L&L PackDefensive · DeterrenceModerate difficulty

Firebrand. When you Copy or Pivot in order to tax, you gain 1 Weapon along with the taxed resource.

Irregular. If a Rival attacks you in battle and is intercepted, they do not take hits from fresh defending ships. Instead, they take 1 hit per Weapon icon you have from cards and resources, then you discard 1 Weapon resource you have.

Setup City on planet A · Starport on planet B (reinforced) · Fuel Material

How to play it

A porcupine with a tax engine. Every off-suit tax stockpiles Weapons, and Weapons do double duty: each is a battle Prelude in your pocket, and each is a warhead wired to Irregular — when an attacker rolls into your defenses and gets intercepted, they eat one hit per Weapon icon you're holding instead of the usual fresh-ship damage. An Agitator sitting on four or five Weapons is functionally unattackable with assault dice; rivals must either skirmish you slowly or leave you alone, and either way you got your game for free.

Play the economy openly and let the arsenal do the deterring. Weapons don't score any ambition directly, so convert the surplus into tempo — Weapon Preludes into opportunistic Warlord strikes — rather than hoarding past the point of deterrence. The one discard per trigger means the porcupine slowly sheds quills when poked; re-tax to reload. Note the errata below: intercept effects you generate also trigger Irregular, which is where the famous pairing lives.

Best lore pairings

Mirror Plating — you add the intercept that detonates Irregular Railgun Arrays — a hit before dice, then the minefield Predictive Sensors — reinforce the ambush they walked into Warlord's Cruelty — re-tax to reload the arsenal mid-war
Errata Irregular now reads "is intercepted" rather than "rolls an intercept" — so an intercept added by Mirror Plating triggers it too. That interaction is intended.

Quartermaster

L&L PackAttrition · InfrastructureBeginner-friendly

Resilient. After any battle with you, repair 1 Loyal ship in the battle system per starport on the map you control. (Even Rival ones!)

Greedy. In setup, place an agent on your Material Outrage slot.

Setup Single starport on planet A with an enlarged home fleet (no second building) · Fuel Weapon

How to play it

The attrition king. Every battle involving you — win, lose, attack, defend — ends with free repairs in that system equal to your starport count, anywhere on the map. With three or four starports under your control, prolonged wars simply don't hurt you: your damaged ships stand back up while the enemy's stay down, and grinding engagements you'd normally avoid become profitable. Fights near your own starports are almost free.

The whole Leader is therefore a starport race. Build them relentlessly, and remember controlled Rival starports count — parking ships on an enemy starport system converts their infrastructure into your field hospital. Greedy locks your Material Prelude at setup, which stings for exactly this plan: you can't spend Material for bonus builds, so your starports come from honest Construction pips (and the Fuel start keeps you mobile in the meantime). One of the easier expansion Leaders to pilot, since the downside is paid once at setup and the upside runs itself.

Best lore pairings

Gate Ports — starports on gates raise the Resilient count Hidden Harbors — fresh forward builds, and raid-proof starports Tyrant's Authority — annex Rival starports permanently Living Structures — Prune cities into starports on demand
Official ruling If Resilient and Repair Drones trigger after the same battle, the player taking the turn chooses the order.

Lore — Base Game

The fourteen Lore cards in the base box. Each is a private technology drafted at setup — and each sits in your play area where a sufficiently lucky raid can steal it.

Tool-Priests

Base GameProduction

Summon (Build): Once per turn, build 1 ship at any city you control. Yes, even Rival cities you control!

How to use it

Pure action compression: every Build pip can now produce a ship at any city you control, including Rival cities you're squatting on — which means frontline production. Instead of building at home and spending two moves to reach the war, Summon ships directly into contested space. It also decouples your fleet growth from your starports, so losing infrastructure hurts far less. Quietly one of the most reliable Lore cards in the box; it's never dead, and in a long game it's dozens of tempo saved.

Best with

Anarchist — you live on Rival cities already Quartermaster — bodies to repair, built where the fighting is Warrior — reinforce the front between strikes

Galactic Rifles

Base GameAggression

Fire Rifles (Battle): Like in a battle, choose a system and collect skirmish dice for fresh Loyal ships there, choose a defender in an adjacent system, roll the collected dice, and deal 1 hit to the defender per hit rolled. (Hit ships before buildings.)

How to use it

Artillery. You bombard an adjacent system with skirmish dice and take zero return fire — no intercepts, no defender hits, no exposure. Use it to soften a garrison the turn before a real assault, to snipe the last damaged ship holding a system, or to punish a rival's staging area without committing your fleet. Because it only counts fresh ships, keep the firebase repaired. It never steals or captures anything, so it's a tempo-and-attrition tool rather than an economy — best in hands that were going to fight anyway.

Best with

Rebel — ranged pressure without moving your capped fleet Agitator — poke from behind the porcupine quills Quartermaster — a firebase that repairs itself

Sprinter Drives

Base GameMobility

How to use it

Doubled reach on every move action, all game long. The fine print is where the value hides: each fresh ship's second move is independent — they don't have to travel together, so one move action can fan a stack out across three systems, or Catapult a fleet and then advance the drop-offs a further step each. Mirror Plating

Base GameDefense

When you defend in battle, add 1 intercept to the attacker's roll if they rolled any number of assault dice.

How to use it

A guaranteed intercept against every assault-dice attack means every fresh defender you have deals a hit back, every time. Attackers respond by rolling skirmish-only against you — slower, weaker attacks — or by not attacking at all, which is the actual point. Deterrence Lore wins by never triggering. Keep your defenders repaired (damaged ships don't punish intercepts) and park value behind the shield. Its stock rises the more aggressive your table is.

Best with

Agitator — the intercept you add detonates Irregular; a table-famous combo Elder — punished attacks that also pay you influence Quartermaster — whatever gets through, you repair

Hidden Harbors

Base GameDefense · Production

You always build ships fresh.

How to use it

Two quiet rules with big consequences. Ships built in Rival-controlled systems normally arrive damaged; yours arrive fresh, so you can grow a real fleet inside contested space — forward starports become instant strongpoints. And a fresh defending starport switches off raid dice entirely, which is body armor for your play area: your Guild cards and resources can't be stolen through those battles. The ideal insurance pick for players planning to be rich.

Best with

Quartermaster — fresh forward builds plus post-battle repairs Archivist — three Lore cards are worth armoring Feastbringer — protects the Guild engine

Signal Breaker

Base GameAggression

How to use it

The alpha-strike enabler. Intercepts are what make assault dice scary; ignoring the first one makes clean, all-fresh attacks dramatically safer. The discipline it asks for is doctrinal: attack with fresh ships only, repair between offensives, and don't drag damaged stragglers into the strike group (one damaged attacker turns the card off for that battle). It rewards exactly the hit-and-run pattern good Arcs aggression already wants.

Best with

Repair Drones

Base GameAggression · Sustain

How to use it

A free repair after every attack you launch turns sustained campaigns from self-destructive into sustainable. The math compounds: over a chapter of five or six battles, that's five or six Repair pips you didn't have to play — and it keeps ships fresh for every "fresh matters" effect in your arsenal. It does nothing on defense and nothing for pacifists, so take it only when your Leader intends to be the aggressor, in which case it's premium.

Gate Ports

How to use it

Toll booths on the galaxy's highways. Gates are the connective tissue of the Reach — everyone must pass through them — and a fresh starport on one takes a captive from every rival who does. Lore — Leaders & Lore Pack

The fourteen expansion Lore cards. Ten of them are "While declared" cards keyed to a specific ambition — they work while anyone has that ambition declared, and five carry a bonus Prelude that discards the card to clear an Outrage.

L&L PackDefense

When defending in battle, before the attacker collects dice, you may move any number of fresh Loyal ships from systems adjacent to the battle system into it.

How to use it

Defensive teleportation. The attacker commits based on what they can see; then your reserves pour in from every adjacent system before a single die is collected, and suddenly the raid math they planned is ruined — more defenders soak more hits and threaten more punishment. Devastating psychological deterrence once your table has seen it fire.

Agitator — bait the strike, then close the trap Elder — attacks on you keep getting worse for them Quartermaster — everyone who piles in gets repaired after

Force Beams

How to use it

The strangest movement in the game: a tractor beam anchored to your fresh starports that moves anyone's ships. Drag a rival's fleet out of a system the turn before scoring, yank a lone enemy ship into your kill zone, tow your own armada without limit — and because Guide ignores move modifiers in play areas, it sails straight past restrictions like the Rebel's Disorganized. Raider Exosuits

L&L PackRaiding

When attacking in battle, if there are no defending buildings, you may collect up to 1 raid die. (This is not an extra die. Follow the limit of 1 die per ship.)

How to use it

Normally raid dice require defending buildings — so players hide their loot behind buildingless fleets in open space or on gates, knowing you can't steal from them there. The Court by Leader

Unlike your drafted Lore, Court cards are contested, arrive by luck of the flip, and can be raided away — so this is about recognizing your cards when they appear, not planning around them. Card text verified against the official card library and the Arcs Fates FAQ.

First, the patterns. The base Court's 25 Guild cards sort into a few families, and knowing the family tells you most of what a card is for. Two facts apply to all of them: every Guild card is a permanent icon of its suit (Material and Fuel score Tycoon, Relic scores Keeper, Psionic scores Empath — Weapon scores nothing, but several effects count Weapon icons), and every Guild card in your play area can be raided, unlike Lore.

The five "Loyal" conversions

(Engineers · Pilots · Marines · Empaths · Keepers) — each lets you spend any resource as its type, ignores Outrage when doing so, and survives your own Provoked Outrage. They decouple your economy from your map position, and they're insurance for anyone planning to crack cities.

The four Unions

(Admin · Construction · Spacing · Arms) — park one next to a played card of the matching suit and recover that card to hand at round's end. Pure card economy: an extra card in hand and a re-run of your best number. Best for Leaders who lean one suit hard.

The two Cartels

(Material · Fuel) — you hold the entire remaining supply of that resource on the card: a Tycoon lock plus denial, since Rivals discard that resource after scoring. Pairs with any engine that mass-produces the matching type.

The two Interests

(Mining · Shipping) — a free resource every time you take a Build action, plus a discard-to-burst Prelude. The builders' engine cards.

The Weapon muscle set

(Prison Wardens · Skirmishers · Court Enforcers · Loyal Marines) — all share the Prelude "discard to place 3 ships in a system you control": an army in a can. Their main abilities scale with your total Weapon icons from cards and resources, which is why Weapon-counting Leaders treasure them.

Declaration support

(Secret Order · Galactic Bards · the Vox card Populist Demands) — declarations without the zero marker, off-initiative, or off-turn. Fuel for every "when you declare" Leader.

Tempo and theft

(Lattice Spies · Silver-Tongues · Farseers) — seize initiative from the Prelude, steal a card or resource outright, or filter your hand. Situational, universally playable.

The vault

(Sworn Guardians) — Rivals cannot steal your resources or other Guild cards; they must raid Guardians first, and it buries itself when taken. The bigger your tableau, the more this is worth.

A word on Vox cards. The six Vox events reward whoever's positioned when they flip: Populist Demands is a bottled declaration — every declare-trigger Leader above should fight for it. Mass Uprising drops a ship in every system of a cluster, which the movement-limited Rebel appreciates more than anyone. Guild Struggle steals a Guild card and recycles the discard pile — the Feastbringer's way of clawing back what Generous gave away. Outrage Spreads forces everyone to Provoke Outrage, which turns asymmetric if you hold a "Loyal" conversion card: you keep yours and spend through the Outrage while rivals eat the full cost. And remember the Demagogue's quiet edge: Paranoid exempts Vox, so his single blanket agents secure these at no extra cost.

How the Guild cards rank. Not 1-to-25 — a strict ordinal list would be false precision, since a Court card's value depends on your Leader, the chapter, and what's declared. Three honest tiers instead, alphabetical within each; the notes mark who spikes a card upward. The base Court has no true duds — even the bottom of B earns its Influence in the right hands.

Galactic Bards (free declarations are tempo, scoring, and trigger-fuel in one; the single most universally wanted card), Lattice Spies (initiative on demand steers the whole round — and it's a Prelude, so it strikes before anyone reacts), Sworn Guardians (turns your entire play area into a vault; scales with everything else you own).

Astrong for nearly everyone.

Bgood with a reason.

Admin / Arms / Construction / Spacing Unions (card economy that scales with how hard you lean one suit — premium for specialists, filler for generalists), Elder Broker (the Prelude burst is real; the Trade helps your rival too), Fuel Cartel and Material Cartel (dead weight without a mass-production engine, oppressive with one), Gatekeepers (map-dependent; jumps a tier for gate fighters like the Rebel and Shaper), Loyal Engineers / Pilots / Empaths / Keepers (conversion flexibility plus Outrage insurance; each jumps a tier when your setup or plan Outrages its type), Relic Fence (steady Keeper income for Relic plans, slow otherwise).

The Vox events, ranked. These can be ordered 1–6, because each is a one-shot effect competing for the same decision — is it worth an agent when it flips? 1. Populist Demands (a bottled declaration — scoring, tempo, and every declare-trigger in the game), 2. Guild Struggle (steal a Guild card and recycle the discard pile into the deck), 3. Mass Uprising (a ship in every system of a cluster — presence no Move action matches), 4. Call to Action (a seventh card is quiet, reliable value), 5. Song of Freedom (initiative plus a city recall; situational but never dead), 6. Outrage Spreads (table-wide chaos — last by default, first if you hold a Loyal conversion card and rivals don't).

The Synergy Web

Some numbers on the pairing graph itself — how likely combos are to appear in your draft, and which Leaders connect to the most Lore.

Will a recommended pair even show up? Almost always. The draft reveals rows of players + 1 cards each, and this guide's recommendations form 87 distinct Leader–Lore links. Computing over every possible layout: at 2 players (rows of 3), at least one recommended pair appears in 87% of drafts, averaging about 1.7 pairs on display; at 3 players (rows of 4), 98% and about 3 pairs; at 4 players (rows of 5), 99.8% and about 5 pairs. Glossary

Terms this guide uses freely — the first group is general board-game vocabulary, the second is Arcs-specific shorthand.

Engine
Any combination of pieces, cards, and abilities that produces value repeatedly without new investment — you set it up once, then it pays out every turn or every trigger. The Fuel-Drinker taxing on every Copy is an engine; so is Tool-Priests summoning a free ship each turn. "Engine-building" play means spending early turns assembling the machine and later turns harvesting it, as opposed to spending each action for one-off value.
Action economy
The count of useful things you get done per card played. Arcs rations actions harshly — Copy and Pivot give only one — so anything granting extra or better actions (Leader riders, Prelude abilities, free builds) is worth more than its face value.
Tempo
Momentum — getting relevant things done sooner than rivals, even at some cost in raw value. A tempo play trades efficiency for speed: hitting a fleet before it repairs, grabbing an ambition marker first, moving now instead of taxing twice.
Broken
Overpowered — not malfunctioning. A "broken engine" is a combination so strong it feels like it breaks the game's balance in your favor.
Combo
Two or more cards whose combined effect is bigger than the sum of the parts — like Mirror Plating auto-intercepting a raid and the Agitator's Irregular triggering off that very intercept.
Tableau
The spread of cards in front of you — in Arcs, your play area of Guild and Lore cards. A "Court-centric" player wins through a big tableau rather than the map.
Deterrence
Value that comes from making attacks unattractive rather than from fighting. Defensive Lore like Railgun Arrays is deterrence: its best games are the ones where nobody dares swing at you.
Turtling
Playing defensively in a shell — hunkering behind defenses and quietly accumulating instead of contesting the map.
Parasite
A strategy that feeds off rivals' development instead of building its own — the Anarchist taxing cities other players constructed is the purest example.
Variance
Swinginess; how far outcomes can stray from the average. Assault dice are high-variance, and rerolls (Seeker Torpedoes) reduce variance. High-variance picks are better when you're behind, worse when you're ahead.
Tier
Informal power ranking of cards or strategies (S/A/B/C in community guides). "Jumps a full tier" means a card plays a class better than usual in the right context.
Meta
The expectations of your particular table — which strategies your group favors and adapts to. A card can be weak in the abstract but strong in your meta.
Banking seconds
Deliberately taking second place in several ambitions rather than fighting for one first. Cheap, quiet Power — which is why Leader clauses forbidding second-place Power are real downsides.
Fresh / damaged (Arcs)
A piece's two states. Fresh pieces stand upright and work fully; a hit tips them damaged, and a second hit destroys them. Many Lore cards only count fresh pieces, which is why the Repair action is bigger than it looks.
Keys (Arcs)
The steal symbol on raid dice. After battle resolves, surviving attackers spend rolled keys to take the defender's resources and Guild cards at their printed raid costs.
Prelude (Arcs)
The window at the start of your card play, before spending pips, when you may use resource abilities and cards marked "Prelude." Prelude actions are pure bonus action economy.
"While declared" (Arcs)
The trigger on the eight ambition-themed pack Lore cards. It switches on when anyone declares that ambition, not just you — a rival's declaration powers your card too.
